<entity_details>

<entity name="sole-proprietorship">
Description: Simplest business structure, no legal separation from owner.

Pros:
- No formation required
- Simple tax filing (Schedule C)
- Complete control
- Minimal paperwork
- Easy to convert to LLC later

Cons:
- No liability protection
- Personal assets at risk
- Limited credibility
- Harder to raise capital
- All business income is self-employment income

Best for:
- Side projects and freelancing
- Testing a business idea
- Very low-risk activities
- Solo operations with minimal liability exposure

Formation requirements: None (may need local business license)
</entity>

<entity name="llc">
Description: Flexible entity providing liability protection with pass-through taxation.

Pros:
- Liability protection for personal assets
- Pass-through taxation (default)
- Flexible profit distribution
- Less formality than corporations
- Can elect S-Corp or C-Corp tax treatment
- No ownership restrictions

Cons:
- Self-employment tax on all profits (unless elect S-Corp)
- Less established legal precedent than corporations
- Some states have franchise tax
- Not ideal for VC funding

Best for:
- Small to medium businesses
- Real estate holding
- Professional services
- Businesses without VC plans
- Multi-member businesses wanting flexibility

Tax treatment options:
- Disregarded entity (single member, pass-through)
- Partnership (multi-member, pass-through)
- S-Corporation election
- C-Corporation election

Formation requirements:
- File Articles of Organization
- Appoint registered agent
- Create Operating Agreement
- Obtain EIN
</entity>

<entity name="s-corporation">
Description: Corporation electing special tax status for pass-through treatment.

Pros:
- Liability protection
- Pass-through taxation (no corporate tax)
- Potential self-employment tax savings
- Established legal structure
- Credibility with some partners

Cons:
- Ownership restrictions (100 shareholders, US only)
- One class of stock only
- Required formalities (minutes, resolutions)
- Reasonable salary requirement
- Not VC compatible (can't have preferred stock)

Best for:
- Profitable small businesses
- Owners paying significant self-employment tax
- Businesses without outside investor plans
- Professional practices

Tax benefit example:
If business earns $200K profit:
- LLC: Pay self-employment tax on full $200K (15.3%)
- S-Corp: Pay salary of $100K (payroll tax), take $100K as distribution (no SE tax)
- Potential savings: ~$15K/year (varies by situation)

Formation requirements:
- Form corporation first (or LLC that elects)
- File Form 2553 with IRS
- Meet all S-Corp requirements
- Maintain formalities
</entity>

<entity name="c-corporation">
Description: Standard corporation, separate tax entity with full flexibility.

Pros:
- Most flexible ownership structure
- Multiple classes of stock allowed
- Required for VC investment
- Established legal framework (especially Delaware)
- Can go public
- Easier to transfer ownership
- Perpetual existence

Cons:
- Double taxation (corporate tax + dividends)
- More formalities required
- Higher administrative costs
- More complex tax filing

Best for:
- Companies seeking VC funding
- Startups planning significant growth
- Businesses that may go public
- Companies wanting complex equity structures
- Businesses retaining significant earnings

QSBS Tax Benefit:
If C-Corp is a Qualified Small Business:
- Exclusion of up to $10M (or 10x basis) of capital gains
- Must hold stock 5+ years
- Significant benefit for successful exits

Delaware advantages:
- Business-friendly court system
- Established case law
- Privacy protections
- Flexibility in governance
- Familiar to investors

Formation requirements:
- File Certificate of Incorporation
- Appoint registered agent
- Create Bylaws
- Hold organizational meeting
- Issue stock
- Obtain EIN
</entity>

</entity_details>

<decision_guidance>

<question name="planning-vc-funding">
If planning to raise venture capital: → C-Corporation (Delaware)
VCs require C-Corp for preferred stock, complex cap tables, and familiar governance.
</question>

<question name="single-owner-service">
If single owner providing services: → Single-member LLC
Simple liability protection without corporate formalities. Can elect S-Corp taxation if profitable enough.
</question>

<question name="multi-owner-equal">
If multiple owners with equal ownership: → Multi-member LLC
Flexibility in profit distribution, simpler than corporation.
</question>

<question name="high-profits-no-investors">
If highly profitable with no outside investment: → LLC with S-Corp election
Get pass-through treatment with self-employment tax savings.
</question>

<question name="just-testing">
If testing an idea with minimal risk: → Start as sole proprietor
Convert to LLC once validated.
</question>

<question name="real-estate">
If holding real estate: → LLC
Liability protection without corporate tax complications.
</question>

</decision_guidance>

<state_considerations>

<state name="delaware">
Best for: C-Corps seeking investment
Why: Business-friendly courts, established law, investor familiarity
Cost: $89 filing + franchise tax
Consideration: May need to foreign qualify in operating state
</state>

<state name="wyoming">
Best for: LLCs seeking privacy
Why: No state income tax, strong privacy, low fees
Cost: $100 filing + $60/year
Consideration: May need to foreign qualify in operating state
</state>

<state name="home-state">
Best for: Local businesses
Why: No foreign qualification needed, local bank relationships
Cost: Varies by state
Consideration: May have less favorable laws than DE/WY
</state>
