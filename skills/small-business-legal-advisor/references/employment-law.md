<worker_classification>

<classification name="employee-vs-contractor">
Why It Matters:
- Tax withholding and reporting
- Benefits eligibility
- Minimum wage and overtime
- Workers' compensation
- Unemployment insurance
- Discrimination protections

IRS Factors (Behavioral, Financial, Relationship):
Behavioral Control:
- Instructions on how to do work
- Training provided
- Set hours and location

Financial Control:
- Investment in equipment
- Opportunity for profit/loss
- Payment method (hourly vs. project)
- Multiple clients

Relationship:
- Written contracts
- Benefits provided
- Permanency of relationship
- Key activity of business
</classification>

<classification name="abc-test">
Applies In: California (AB5), Massachusetts, New Jersey, others
Contractor Status Requires ALL:
A) Free from control and direction
B) Performing work outside usual course of hiring entity's business
C) Engaged in independently established trade or business

Most Restrictive: Prong B is hardest to satisfy
Example Failure: Software company hiring software developer as contractor
</classification>

<classification name="misclassification-penalties">
IRS Penalties:
- Back employment taxes
- Penalties (1.5%-3% of wages)
- 100% of employee share if willful

State Penalties:
- Back unemployment insurance
- Workers' compensation premiums
- State employment taxes
- Additional state penalties

Other Consequences:
- Employee lawsuits (overtime, benefits)
- Class action exposure
- DOL investigations
</classification>

</worker_classification>

<hiring_process>

<stage name="job-posting">
Legal Requirements:
- No discriminatory language
- Salary range disclosure (some states/cities)
- Accurate job requirements
- Equal opportunity statement

Avoid:
- Age preferences ("young," "recent grad")
- Gender preferences
- Physical requirements not job-related
- Citizenship requirements (unless job-specific)
</stage>

<stage name="interviewing">
Prohibited Questions:
- Age or date of birth
- Marital status, children, pregnancy
- Race, ethnicity, national origin
- Religion
- Disability (before offer)
- Genetic information
- Criminal history (timing varies by location)

Allowed Questions:
- Work experience
- Skills and qualifications
- Availability
- Ability to perform essential functions
- Authorization to work in US
</stage>

<stage name="background-checks">
FCRA Requirements:
- Written authorization
- Pre-adverse action notice
- Copy of report to candidate
- Adverse action notice with rights

Ban-the-Box Laws:
- Many jurisdictions restrict timing of criminal history inquiry
- Often can't ask until conditional offer
- Must consider relevance to job

State-Specific Limits:
- Credit check restrictions (some states)
- Social media password restrictions
- Salary history bans
</stage>

<stage name="offer-and-onboarding">
Offer Letter Elements:
- Position and start date
- Compensation and benefits
- At-will statement (if applicable)
- Contingencies (background check, references)
- Acceptance deadline

Required Forms:
- I-9 (within 3 days of start)
- W-4 (federal withholding)
- State withholding forms
- Benefits enrollment
- Policy acknowledgments
</stage>

</hiring_process>

<compensation_and_benefits>

<requirement name="minimum-wage">
Federal Minimum: $7.25/hour (as of knowledge cutoff)
State/Local Minimums: Often higher (check applicable rate)
Tipped Employees: Lower minimum + tip credit (varies by state)
Exempt Employees: Salary minimum for exemption
</requirement>

<requirement name="overtime">
FLSA Requirements:
- Non-exempt employees: 1.5x regular rate over 40 hours/week
- Salary alone doesn't determine exemption
- Must meet duties test for exemption

Exempt Categories:
- Executive: Manage enterprise/department, supervise 2+
- Administrative: Office work, exercise discretion
- Professional: Advanced knowledge, intellectual work
- Computer: Systems analyst, programmer at minimum salary
- Outside Sales: Away from office, making sales

Salary Threshold: Check current DOL threshold for exemption
</requirement>

<requirement name="equal-pay">
Equal Pay Act: Equal pay for equal work regardless of sex
State Laws: Many broader (race, etc.)
Pay Equity Audits: Recommended practice
Salary History Bans: Many states prohibit asking
</requirement>

<requirement name="benefits">
Required (Employer Size Dependent):
- Social Security/Medicare
- Workers' compensation
- Unemployment insurance
- FMLA leave (50+ employees)
- ACA health coverage (50+ full-time)

Optional:
- Health insurance
- Retirement plans
- Paid time off
- Life/disability insurance
- Stock options/equity
</requirement>

</compensation_and_benefits>

<workplace_policies>

<policy name="employee-handbook">
Essential Policies:
- At-will employment statement
- Equal employment opportunity
- Anti-harassment/discrimination
- Complaint procedure
- Time off policies
- Conduct standards
- Drug and alcohol policy
- Electronic communication policy
- Acknowledgment page

Caution Areas:
- Policies that create implied contracts
- Inconsistent enforcement
- Overly broad confidentiality
- NLRA-protected activity restrictions
</policy>

<policy name="anti-harassment">
Elements:
- Definition of harassment
- Examples of prohibited conduct
- Reporting procedures
- Investigation process
- Non-retaliation commitment
- Training requirements

Training Requirements:
- California: 2 hours (supervisors), 1 hour (employees)
- New York: Annual training
- Other states: Varies
</policy>

<policy name="leave-policies">
Federal FMLA (50+ employees):
- 12 weeks unpaid leave
- Serious health condition
- New child
- Military family leave
- Job protection

State Leave Laws:
- Often more generous
- May cover smaller employers
- Paid leave requirements increasing
</policy>

</workplace_policies>

<termination>

<principle name="at-will-employment">
General Rule: Either party can end employment at any time
Exceptions:
- Discrimination (protected class)
- Retaliation (whistleblowing, complaints)
- Breach of contract
- Public policy violation
</principle>

<process name="termination-best-practices">
Before Termination:
- Document performance issues
- Consistent enforcement
- Final decision by appropriate level
- Consider disparate impact
- Review any agreements

During Termination:
- Private meeting, brief
- Clear explanation
- Witness present
- Return of property
- Final paycheck (timing varies by state)

After Termination:
- COBRA notice (if applicable)
- Unemployment claim response
- Reference policy
- Exit interview (optional)
</process>

<consideration name="final-pay">
State Rules Vary:
- California: Immediately upon involuntary termination
- Many states: Next regular payday
- Accrued PTO: Depends on policy and state law

Common Issues:
- Late payment penalties
- Deduction disputes
- Commission payment timing
</consideration>

<consideration name="separation-agreements">
When to Use:
- Risk of claims
- Confidential termination
- Departing executive

Key Terms:
- Release of claims
- Consideration (severance)
- OWBPA compliance (over 40)
- Non-disparagement
- Confidentiality
- Return of property
- Cooperation
</consideration>

</termination>

<common_pitfalls>

<pitfall name="unpaid-internships">
Risk: May be employees entitled to minimum wage
Legal Test: Primary beneficiary test (educational benefit)
Safe Approach: Pay interns at least minimum wage
</pitfall>

<pitfall name="off-clock-work">
Risk: Pre/post shift work, working through lunch
Examples: Opening/closing duties, answering emails
Solution: Clear policy, enforce breaks, track all time
</pitfall>

<pitfall name="exempt-misclassification">
Risk: Treating non-exempt as exempt to avoid overtime
Common Mistake: Job title doesn't determine exemption
Solution: Analyze actual duties against exemption tests
</pitfall>

</common_pitfalls>
