<overview>
Plain language explanations of legal terms and concepts commonly encountered by small businesses.
</overview>

<corporate_concepts>

<term name="limited-liability">
Definition: Legal protection that separates personal assets from business debts and obligations.
Plain English: If your business gets sued or can't pay its bills, creditors generally can't take your house, car, or personal savings.
Key Points:
- Only applies to properly formed entities (LLCs, corporations)
- Can be lost through "piercing the corporate veil"
- Requires maintaining separation between personal and business finances
- Does not protect against personal guarantees you've signed
</term>

<term name="piercing-the-corporate-veil">
Definition: When courts disregard the liability protection of a business entity.
Plain English: If you treat your business like a personal piggy bank, courts may hold you personally responsible for business debts.
Common Triggers:
- Mixing personal and business funds
- Not maintaining corporate formalities
- Undercapitalizing the business
- Using the entity for fraud
Prevention: Keep separate accounts, hold meetings, maintain records, adequate capitalization.
</term>

<term name="fiduciary-duty">
Definition: Legal obligation to act in the best interest of another party.
Plain English: A higher standard of care where you must put someone else's interests first.
Examples:
- Directors owe fiduciary duty to shareholders
- Trustees owe fiduciary duty to beneficiaries
- Employees in certain roles owe duty to employers
Components: Duty of care, duty of loyalty, duty of good faith
</term>

<term name="articles-of-organization">
Definition: The document filed with the state to legally create an LLC.
Plain English: The LLC's birth certificate - the basic document that brings your LLC into existence.
Contents: LLC name, registered agent, principal office, management structure, organizer signatures.
Also Known As: Certificate of Organization (some states)
</term>

<term name="operating-agreement">
Definition: Internal document governing how an LLC operates and members' rights.
Plain English: The rulebook for your LLC that explains who owns what and who decides what.
Key Provisions: Ownership percentages, voting rights, profit distribution, management structure, transfer restrictions.
Note: Not filed with state but essential for operations.
</term>

<term name="registered-agent">
Definition: Person or entity designated to receive legal documents on behalf of a business.
Plain English: The official address where someone can serve your company with legal papers.
Requirements: Must have physical address in state of formation, available during business hours.
Options: Owner, employee, or commercial registered agent service.
</term>

<term name="foreign-qualification">
Definition: Registering your business to operate in a state other than where it was formed.
Plain English: If you formed your LLC in Delaware but operate in California, you need to register in California too.
Triggers: Physical presence, employees, significant sales, or property in another state.
Cost: Filing fees plus ongoing registered agent in each state.
</term>

</corporate_concepts>

<contract_concepts>

<term name="consideration">
Definition: Something of value exchanged by parties to make a contract enforceable.
Plain English: Each side has to give up something for a contract to be valid - can't just be a one-way gift.
Examples: Money, services, goods, promises to do or not do something.
Key Point: Courts don't judge if consideration is "fair" - just that it exists.
</term>

<term name="indemnification">
Definition: Agreement by one party to compensate another for certain losses or damages.
Plain English: "If something goes wrong, I'll cover your costs."
Types:
- Mutual: Both parties agree to cover the other
- One-way: Only one party agrees to cover
Common Use: Protecting against third-party claims
</term>

<term name="limitation-of-liability">
Definition: Contract clause that caps the maximum damages one party can recover.
Plain English: Setting a ceiling on how much you'd have to pay if something goes wrong.
Types:
- Liability caps (e.g., limited to fees paid)
- Exclusion of certain damages (e.g., no consequential damages)
Note: Some limitations may not be enforceable depending on jurisdiction and circumstances.
</term>

<term name="force-majeure">
Definition: Contract clause excusing performance due to extraordinary events beyond control.
Plain English: The "Act of God" clause - what happens when something truly unexpected makes performance impossible.
Typical Events: Natural disasters, war, pandemic, government actions.
Negotiation Point: Specifically list covered events; general language may not be enforceable.
</term>

<term name="assignment">
Definition: Transfer of contract rights or obligations to another party.
Plain English: Passing your contract duties or benefits to someone else.
Key Provisions:
- Can be prohibited entirely
- May require consent
- May be permitted without restriction
Note: Assignment of duties usually doesn't release the original party unless there's a novation.
</term>

<term name="severability">
Definition: Provision allowing the rest of a contract to remain valid if part is unenforceable.
Plain English: If a court strikes down one clause, the rest of the contract survives.
Importance: Protects your overall agreement from being voided due to one problematic provision.
</term>

<term name="governing-law">
Definition: Clause specifying which jurisdiction's laws interpret the contract.
Plain English: Which state's (or country's) rules apply if there's a dispute.
Consideration: Choose a jurisdiction with favorable laws and precedent for your situation.
</term>

<term name="representations-and-warranties">
Definition: Statements of fact and promises about the truth of those facts in a contract.
Representation: A statement of existing fact.
Warranty: A promise that the statement is and will remain true.
Plain English: "Here's what I'm telling you is true, and I stand behind it."
Risk: False representations can lead to breach claims and damages.
</term>

</contract_concepts>

<ip_concepts>

<term name="trademark">
Definition: Word, phrase, symbol, or design identifying the source of goods or services.
Plain English: Your brand name, logo, or slogan that customers associate with your business.
Protection: Exclusive right to use the mark in your industry; prevent confusingly similar marks.
Registration: Not required but provides significant legal benefits (federal registration).
Duration: Indefinite if maintained and renewed.
</term>

<term name="copyright">
Definition: Protection for original works of authorship fixed in tangible form.
Plain English: Automatic protection for creative works - writing, code, art, music, etc.
What It Covers: Expression of ideas, not the ideas themselves.
Work for Hire: Employers typically own copyright in employee work.
Duration: Life of author plus 70 years (varies for different situations).
Registration: Not required for protection, but required to sue and get statutory damages.
</term>

<term name="patent">
Definition: Exclusive right to make, use, or sell an invention for a limited time.
Plain English: Government-granted monopoly on your invention in exchange for public disclosure.
Types: Utility (functional), Design (appearance), Plant (new plant varieties).
Requirements: Novel, non-obvious, useful, and properly disclosed.
Duration: 20 years from filing (utility), 15 years from grant (design).
Cost: Typically $15K-$30K+ for utility patent through prosecution.
</term>

<term name="trade-secret">
Definition: Information with economic value from being kept secret.
Plain English: Business secrets you actively protect that give you a competitive advantage.
Examples: Customer lists, formulas, algorithms, processes, supplier information.
Protection: NDAs, restricted access, security measures.
Duration: Indefinite, as long as secrecy is maintained.
No Registration: Protection comes from taking reasonable steps to maintain secrecy.
</term>

<term name="work-for-hire">
Definition: Work created by employees or contractors that belongs to the commissioning party.
Plain English: When you hire someone to create something, who owns it?
Employee Work: Generally employer owns by default.
Contractor Work: Must have written agreement; only certain categories qualify as work for hire.
Important: Always get written IP assignment from contractors for work outside statutory categories.
</term>

<term name="ip-assignment">
Definition: Transfer of intellectual property rights from one party to another.
Plain English: Signing over ownership of creative work or inventions to someone else.
When Used: Contractors transferring work to client, founders contributing IP to company.
Requirements: Must be in writing, especially for copyright and patents.
Present Assignment: "I hereby assign" vs. "I agree to assign" - important distinction.
</term>

</ip_concepts>

<liability_concepts>

<term name="negligence">
Definition: Failure to exercise reasonable care, resulting in damage to another.
Plain English: Being careless in a way that hurts someone else.
Elements: Duty, breach of duty, causation, damages.
Business Context: Product liability, slip-and-fall, professional malpractice.
</term>

<term name="breach-of-contract">
Definition: Failure to perform contractual obligations without legal excuse.
Plain English: Breaking your promise in a contract.
Types:
- Material breach: Serious, may terminate contract
- Minor breach: Lesser, damages only
Remedies: Damages, specific performance, rescission.
</term>

<term name="strict-liability">
Definition: Liability without fault - responsibility regardless of intent or negligence.
Plain English: You're responsible even if you did everything right.
Common Areas: Product defects, abnormally dangerous activities, certain environmental harms.
</term>

<term name="joint-and-several-liability">
Definition: When multiple parties are each fully responsible for an entire obligation.
Plain English: If you're one of three partners in a liability, a creditor can come after you for the full amount.
Business Context: General partnerships, some guarantees, certain tortious conduct.
</term>

</liability_concepts>

<employment_concepts>

<term name="at-will-employment">
Definition: Employment that either party can terminate at any time for any legal reason.
Plain English: You can quit anytime; employer can fire you anytime (with exceptions).
Exceptions: Discrimination, retaliation, breach of contract, public policy violations.
States: Montana is the only non-at-will state.
</term>

<term name="independent-contractor">
Definition: Worker who provides services but is not an employee.
Plain English: Someone who works for you but runs their own business.
Key Factors: Control over how work is done, financial independence, relationship nature.
Misclassification Risk: Penalties, back taxes, benefits liability if classified incorrectly.
</term>

<term name="non-compete-agreement">
Definition: Contract restricting an employee from working for competitors after leaving.
Plain English: Agreement that you won't go work for the competition for a period of time.
Enforceability: Varies widely by state; California generally doesn't enforce them.
Requirements: Reasonable time, geographic scope, protected interest.
</term>

<term name="non-solicitation-agreement">
Definition: Contract restricting contact with clients or employees after leaving.
Plain English: Agreement not to poach customers or coworkers when you leave.
Generally More Enforceable: Than non-competes because less restrictive.
Types: Customer non-solicitation, employee non-solicitation.
</term>

</employment_concepts>

<regulatory_concepts>

<term name="compliance">
Definition: Conforming to laws, regulations, guidelines, and specifications.
Plain English: Following the rules that apply to your business.
Areas: Employment, data privacy, industry-specific, tax, corporate.
Approach: Identify applicable rules, implement controls, monitor, document.
</term>

<term name="due-diligence">
Definition: Reasonable investigation before entering into a transaction or agreement.
Plain English: Doing your homework before making a major business decision.
Contexts: Acquisitions, investments, major contracts, hiring.
Purpose: Identify risks, verify representations, inform decision-making.
</term>

<term name="data-breach">
Definition: Unauthorized access to or disclosure of protected information.
Plain English: When someone who shouldn't have your data gets access to it.
Obligations: Notification requirements vary by state and type of data.
Prevention: Security measures, access controls, encryption, training.
</term>

</regulatory_concepts>
